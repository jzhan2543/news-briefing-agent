# EU AI Act Enforcement Relies on Decentralized National Authorities with Record-Breaking Penalties

The EU AI Act establishes a decentralized enforcement framework requiring each member state to designate national authorities while imposing fines that exceed even GDPR penalties. Member states are adopting varied governance models, with most dispersing oversight across existing sectoral regulators while a few create centralized bodies.

## Penalty Structure and Enforcement Framework

The EU AI Act empowers member states to establish their own penalty rules within a tiered framework that surpasses GDPR sanctions [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. The most severe penalties apply to prohibited AI systems, with fines reaching up to €35 million or 7% of annual worldwide turnover for companies [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act]. Each member state must designate at least one national authority to oversee implementation and conduct market surveillance [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1].

## National Implementation Approaches

Member states are pursuing divergent governance models for AI Act enforcement. The term "national competent authority" serves as an umbrella designation encompassing both notifying authorities and market surveillance authorities, with each member state required to establish at least one of each as a single point of contact [source: https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/who-governs-ai-eu-breakdown-authorities-eu-ai-act].

France exemplifies the decentralized approach, designating multiple market surveillance authorities divided by sector and AI system type without modifying existing national laws [source: https://www.technologyslegaledge.com/2025/11/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-implementation-in-key-member-states/]. Under the French model, the data protection authority CNIL will enforce rules on prohibited emotion recognition systems in workplaces and educational institutions, while the health products agency ANSM will oversee high-risk medical device AI systems [source: https://www.technologyslegaledge.com/2025/11/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-implementation-in-key-member-states/].

Among 19 countries that have designated or proposed governance models, the dominant pattern involves dispersing market surveillance duties among existing sectoral regulators such as data protection offices, financial supervisors, telecommunications regulators, and health agencies [source: https://www.bsg.ox.ac.uk/blog/ai-acts-enforcement-gap-what-polands-new-regulator-reveals-about-europes-challenge]. Poland stands as a notable exception by creating a brand-new centralized authority specifically for AI Act enforcement [source: https://www.bsg.ox.ac.uk/blog/ai-acts-enforcement-gap-what-polands-new-regulator-reveals-about-europes-challenge].

## Coordination and Harmonization Challenges

While the AI Act establishes a harmonizing framework with direct legal effect across all EU member states, several provisions require national transposition [source: https://www.deloitte.com/dl/en/services/legal/perspectives/nationale-umsetzung-eu-ai-act.html — though specific implementation details are not independently verified]. Market surveillance authorities designated by each member state hold varied responsibilities for overseeing markets and enforcing AI Act compliance [source: https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/who-governs-ai-eu-breakdown-authorities-eu-ai-act].

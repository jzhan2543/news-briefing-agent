# US Tariff Policy Undergoes Major Shifts Following Supreme Court Ruling and Administrative Changes

## Executive Summary

US tariff policy experienced significant upheaval in early 2026 following a Supreme Court ruling that struck down tariffs imposed under emergency powers, prompting the Trump administration to implement replacement tariffs under alternative legal authorities. The effective US tariff rate reached its highest level since 1947, with current policy representing an 11.3 percentage point increase in the average effective tariff rate while temporary measures remain in effect [source: https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-february-21-2026]. The administration is pursuing a dual strategy of maintaining elevated tariffs while negotiating new trade agreements that require partner countries to lower their barriers to US exports in exchange for modified US tariff rates [source: https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20Trade%20Policy%20Agenda.pdf].

## Supreme Court Ruling and Policy Response

The US Supreme Court issued a 6-3 ruling declaring tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) illegal, forcing the administration to restructure its tariff framework [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling]. In response, the Trump administration moved quickly to preserve tariff levels under alternative legal authorities, with US customs authorities stopping collection of IEEPA duties just over three days after the ruling [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling].

The administration implemented a temporary 10% global import tariff under Section 122 authority to last 150 days, though officials indicated they were working to increase it to 15% [source: https://www.reuters.com/business/new-us-tariffs-come-lower-10-rate-2026-02-24/]. President Trump publicly stated his intention to raise global tariffs to 15% following the Supreme Court decision [source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/21/trump-tariff-reactions]. An executive order dated February 20, 2026 formally addressed changes to certain tariff actions, including modifications to reciprocal tariffs and country-specific rates [source: https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/02/ending-certain-tariff-actions/].

## Current Tariff Levels and Economic Impact

Current tariff policy, while Section 122 tariffs remain in effect, represents an 11.3 percentage point increase in the US average effective tariff rate measured pre-substitution [source: https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-february-21-2026]. If the Section 122 tariffs expire in 150 days as scheduled, the effective tariff rate will be 9.1%, which would still represent the highest level since 1947 [source: https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-february-21-2026].

The Tax Foundation estimates that Section 232 tariffs and the 10% Section 122 tariffs will increase federal tax revenues by $81 billion in 2026, or 0.26% of GDP, ranking as the 20th largest tax increase since 1940 [source: https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/trump-tariffs-trade-war/]. Over the longer term, Section 232 tariffs alone are projected to increase US federal tax revenue by $635 billion from 2026 through 2035 on a conventional basis [source: https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/trump-tariffs-trade-war/].

The Budget Lab at Yale projects that the current tariff regime will increase the unemployment rate by 0.3 percentage points by the end of 2026, all else equal [source: https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-february-21-2026].

## Trade Agreement Strategy

The administration's 2026 Trade Policy Agenda outlines a strategy combining tariffs with new trade agreements to reshape US import profiles and support domestic production [source: https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20Trade%20Policy%20Agenda.pdf]. These agreements, described as legally-binding and fully enforceable, require trading partners to significantly lower their tariffs and non-tariff barriers to US exports while the United States maintains a modified tariff on the trading partner [source: https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20Trade%20Policy%20Agenda.pdf].

However, the Supreme Court ruling has complicated implementation of these agreements, with some foreign officials publicly stating their legislatures have not yet ratified recent trade deals with Washington that were crafted with the now-voided tariffs in mind [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling].

## International Reactions

Global reactions to the tariff changes have been mixed. For major South Korean companies in chemicals, pharmaceuticals, and semiconductors, the Supreme Court ruling was viewed positively, as even with new 10% tariffs under Section 122, they would pay lower rates than under the previous regime [source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/21/trump-tariff-reactions]. India welcomed the reduction of tariffs to 18% and removal of penal duties on Russian imports, though uncertainty remains over negotiations given the changed legal basis [source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/21/trump-tariff-reactions].

The Chinese embassy in Washington issued a statement noting that trade wars benefit nobody, with the decision likely broadly welcomed in China, which has been a primary target of Trump's tariff policies [source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/21/trump-tariff-reactions]. The Supreme Court ruling has emboldened US trade partners more broadly as they assess the policy fallout [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling].

## Impact on EU-US Trade

Analysis from ING projects that the direct tariff impact, shifts in relative competitiveness, and uncertainty-related effects point to a net reduction of around 4.2% in EU-US exports in 2025 [source: https://think.ing.com/articles/the-us-tariff-shock-same-negative-impact-different-drivers-in-2025-and-2026/]. The 2026 projection assumes conclusion of a trade agreement between the EU and US, leading to a slight increase in effectively paid tariffs whose economic impact is expected to strengthen gradually over time [source: https://think.ing.com/articles/the-us-tariff-shock-same-negative-impact-different-drivers-in-2025-and-2026/].

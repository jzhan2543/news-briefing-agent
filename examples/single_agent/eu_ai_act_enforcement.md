# EU AI Act Enforcement Faces Implementation Delays as Member States Struggle to Meet Authority Designation Deadlines

## Executive Summary

The EU AI Act, formally adopted in 2024, establishes one of the world's most stringent AI regulatory frameworks with penalties reaching €35 million or 7% of global turnover for the most severe violations [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act]. However, implementation across member states has encountered significant obstacles, with almost no EU member state having fulfilled obligations to enact the legislation and designate competent national authorities despite approaching deadlines [source: https://edri.org/our-work/open-letter-european-commission-member-states-keep-ai-act-national-implementation-on-track/]. The decentralized enforcement model requires each member state to establish its own regulatory infrastructure by August 2, 2025, while the bulk of provisions governing high-risk AI systems become fully applicable on August 2, 2026 [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory] [source: https://acuvity.ai/the-eu-ai-act-what-it-means-for-companies-developing-and-using-ai/].

## Regulatory Framework and Objectives

The AI Act was adopted by the European Parliament and the Council in 2024 with multiple objectives including improving the functioning of the internal market, promoting human-centric and trustworthy AI, ensuring protection against harmful AI systems, and supporting innovation [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/]. The legislation has broad extraterritorial reach similar to the GDPR, extending its compliance requirements beyond EU borders [source: https://www.barradvisory.com/resource/eu-ai-act-2026/]. The Act is being implemented in phases through 2026, with the main requirements, particularly those governing high-risk AI systems, becoming fully applicable on August 2, 2026—24 months after the Act's entry into force [source: https://www.barradvisory.com/resource/eu-ai-act-2026/] [source: https://acuvity.ai/the-eu-ai-act-what-it-means-for-companies-developing-and-using-ai/].

## Decentralized Enforcement Structure

The EU AI Act employs decentralized enforcement by empowering member states to establish their own rules on penalties and administrative fines [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. Each member state must designate at least one national authority to oversee implementation and market surveillance of the AI Act [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. More specifically, the Act requires member states to establish or designate at least one notifying authority and at least one market surveillance authority, with the flexibility to designate more than one of each type to match their organizational needs [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory]. The term 'national competent authority' serves as an overarching collective term that encompasses both 'notifying authorities' and 'market surveillance authorities,' with these designated authorities serving as a single point of contact for the purposes of the AI Act [source: https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/who-governs-ai-eu-breakdown-authorities-eu-ai-act].

Enforcement responsibilities are divided between the EU's AI Office, which handles general-purpose AI systems, and national regulators, who oversee all other AI systems [source: https://www.spektr.com/blog/eu-ai-act-timeline-enforcement-fines-and-how-to-prepare]. The European Commission maintains a centralized list of single points of contact across all EU member states regarding AI Act enforcement [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/].

## Penalty Structure and Financial Consequences

The EU AI Act establishes a tiered penalty system that represents one of the most stringent regulatory frameworks in the EU from a financial enforcement perspective. The fine structure is divided into levels based on the severity of the breach [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. At the lower end, providing incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information carries penalties up to €7,500,000 or 1% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act]. Breaches of high-risk AI requirements can result in fines up to €15 million or 3% of global turnover [source: https://www.spektr.com/blog/eu-ai-act-timeline-enforcement-fines-and-how-to-prepare].

The most severe penalties apply to prohibited AI systems: using or making available prohibited AI systems carries the highest fines under the EU AI Act—up to €35,000,000 or 7% of annual worldwide turnover [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act]. Non-compliance with prohibited AI systems under Article 5 can result in penalties up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher [source: https://www.spektr.com/blog/eu-ai-act-timeline-enforcement-fines-and-how-to-prepare]. Notably, the maximum fines under the EU AI Act exceed those provided for in the GDPR, which are capped at €20 million, making it one of the heftiest penalty regimes for non-compliance in the EU [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1] [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act].

## Implementation Progress Across Member States

Despite the approaching deadline of August 2, 2025, for all EU member states to designate or establish their national competent authorities [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory], implementation progress has been severely lacking. Almost no EU member state has fulfilled obligations to enact the AI Act and designate competent national authorities despite the deadline expiring [source: https://edri.org/our-work/open-letter-european-commission-member-states-keep-ai-act-national-implementation-on-track/]. This concern has prompted 31 civil society organizations to sign an open letter expressing alarm about the lack of AI Act implementation progress [source: https://edri.org/our-work/open-letter-european-commission-member-states-keep-ai-act-national-implementation-on-track/].

The Commission's consolidated list reveals varying levels of implementation progress: nine authorities appear in the list for National Competent Authorities, while twenty authorities appear in the list for authorities protecting fundamental rights [source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/national-implementation-plans/]. Some countries have designated numerous authorities while others are still in the planning stages [source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/national-implementation-plans/].

### National Approaches: France and Germany

France has adopted a distinctive approach to implementation. Rather than modifying existing national laws to adapt them to the EU AI Act, France is drafting a national law to designate enforcement and oversight powers [source: https://www.technologyslegaledge.com/2025/11/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-implementation-in-key-member-states/]. France has adopted a decentralized enforcement model with multiple market surveillance authorities divided by sector and type of AI system [source: https://www.technologyslegaledge.com/2025/11/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-implementation-in-key-member-states/]. Under this structure, the French data protection authority (CNIL) will enforce rules on prohibited AI systems related to emotion recognition in workplaces and educational institutions, while France's National Agency for the Safety of Medicines and Health Products (ANSM) will oversee high-risk AI systems related to medical devices [source: https://www.technologyslegaledge.com/2025/11/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-implementation-in-key-member-states/].

In Germany, the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action and the Ministry for Justice are jointly responsible for implementing the AI Act [source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/national-implementation-plans/]. The Federal Network Agency is expected to be designated as Germany's market surveillance authority and the Federal Accreditation body as the notifying authority [source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/national-implementation-plans/].

## Concerns About Centralization and Deregulation

The implementation landscape faces additional uncertainty due to proposed changes at the EU level. The digital omnibus on AI, proposed by the Commission in November 2025, is a set of amendments designed to further centralize enforcement of the AI Act [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/]. However, civil society organizations have raised concerns that the European Commission's planned digital omnibus threatens to deregulate and delay the implementation of the AI Act [source: https://edri.org/our-work/open-letter-european-commission-member-states-keep-ai-act-national-implementation-on-track/], creating tension between centralization efforts and implementation progress.

## Organizational Compliance Readiness

The strict enforcement framework requires compliance teams to proactively assess their AI systems and ensure alignment with the Act [source: https://www.spektr.com/blog/eu-ai-act-timeline-enforcement-fines-and-how-to-prepare]. Organizations must conduct AI risk assessments and map AI systems to risk categories for compliance, while strengthening data governance and documentation practices and implementing transparency and oversight controls [source: https://www.barradvisory.com/resource/eu-ai-act-2026/]. Creating an inventory of AI systems is the foundation for compliance, as companies cannot manage risk or address gaps without knowing what AI they are using [source: https://acuvity.ai/the-eu-ai-act-what-it-means-for-companies-developing-and-using-ai/]. Companies must assess each AI use case to determine if the system's output impacts people in ways the Act deems high-risk, such as affecting their safety or rights [source: https://acuvity.ai/the-eu-ai-act-what-it-means-for-companies-developing-and-using-ai/].

An organization's readiness for EU AI Act compliance depends on how well it is already prepared in underlying compliance fields such as privacy and cybersecurity [source: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/organizations-face-challenges-in-timely-compliance-with-the-eu-ai-act/]. Leading organizations, particularly in highly regulated industries, already have the foundations in place to meet EU AI Act compliance requirements including governance, definitions, and tooling, and established players with compliance teams should be able to manage EU AI Act requirements effectively [source: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/organizations-face-challenges-in-timely-compliance-with-the-eu-ai-act/]. However, companies that are already behind in their AI journeys may find it daunting to navigate the new EU AI Act regulations [source: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/organizations-face-challenges-in-timely-compliance-with-the-eu-ai-act/].

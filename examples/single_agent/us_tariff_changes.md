# US Tariff Policy in 2026: Supreme Court Ruling Triggers Policy Reset Amid Ongoing Trade Restructuring

## Executive Summary

US tariff policy in 2026 has been marked by significant legal and administrative upheaval following a Supreme Court decision that struck down major Trump-era tariffs, prompting the administration to implement replacement measures under alternative legal authorities. The administration has pursued a dual strategy of maintaining protective tariffs while negotiating new reciprocal trade agreements, with current policies projected to raise over $1 trillion in revenue through 2035 while imposing substantial costs on American businesses and consumers. International reactions have been mixed, with some trading partners benefiting from reduced rates while others await clarity on the legal and diplomatic framework governing future trade relations.

## Supreme Court Invalidation and Policy Response

A pivotal Supreme Court ruling fundamentally reshaped US tariff policy in early 2026. The Court ruled 6-3 that Trump's tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) were illegal [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling]. The decision declared most Trump tariffs illegal, creating significant uncertainty in global trade policy [source: https://www.latimes.com/world-nation/story/2026-02-21/global-leaders-businesses-react-to-more-u-s-tariff-swings].

In response to this legal setback, the administration moved quickly to preserve tariff measures under alternative authorities. An executive order was issued on February 20, 2026, addressing changes to certain tariff actions originally implemented by the Trump administration [source: https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/02/ending-certain-tariff-actions/]. The White House attempted to preserve the tariffs under other tariff authorities following the Supreme Court ruling [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling].

The administration implemented a new temporary global import tariff designed to last 150 days, replacing the broad duties under the emergency law that were struck down by the Supreme Court [source: https://www.reuters.com/business/new-us-tariffs-come-lower-10-rate-2026-02-24/]. However, the rollout was marked by confusion: Trump initially signed an order for a 10% tariff on Friday but announced an increase to 15% on Saturday [source: https://www.reuters.com/business/new-us-tariffs-come-lower-10-rate-2026-02-24/]. The United States began collecting the temporary new 10% global import tariff on Tuesday, with the Trump administration working to increase the rate from 10% to 15% [source: https://www.reuters.com/business/new-us-tariffs-come-lower-10-rate-2026-02-24/].

Following the Supreme Court decision, Trump announced new global tariffs of 15 percent on imported goods [source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/21/trump-tariff-reactions].

## Strategic Trade Policy Framework

Beyond the immediate legal challenges, the administration has articulated a broader strategic vision for trade policy. The United States is using a combination of tariffs and trade agreements to bring back domestic production needed for economic competitiveness and national security [source: https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20Trade%20Policy%20Agenda.pdf]. Trade policy is supporting both an increase in U.S. exports and the expansion of domestic production while changing the United States' import profile [source: https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20Trade%20Policy%20Agenda.pdf].

A key innovation in this approach is the introduction of Agreements on Reciprocal Trade (ART). Each ART agreement is legally-binding and fully enforceable, requiring trading partners to significantly lower tariffs and non-tariff barriers to U.S. exports [source: https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20Trade%20Policy%20Agenda.pdf]. Under ART agreements, the United States maintains a modified tariff on the trading partner in exchange for their reduced barriers to American goods [source: https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20Trade%20Policy%20Agenda.pdf].

## Fiscal Impact and Revenue Projections

The tariff policies are expected to generate substantial federal revenue. The administration's tariffs will raise approximately $1.3 trillion over 2026-35, though slower economic growth reduces net dynamic revenue to $1.1 trillion [source: https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-february-21-2026]. More specifically, Section 232 tariffs are estimated to increase US federal tax revenue by $635 billion from 2026 through 2035 on a conventional basis [source: https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/trump-tariffs-trade-war/].

In the near term, the Section 232 tariffs and 10 percent Section 122 tariffs combined will increase federal tax revenues by $81 billion in 2026, representing 0.26 percent of GDP [source: https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/trump-tariffs-trade-war/]. The 2026 tariff-related revenue increase ranks as the 20th largest tax increase since 1940 [source: https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/trump-tariffs-trade-war/]. Notably, the IEEPA tariff has been ruled illegal and is not included in current revenue projections [source: https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/trump-tariffs-trade-war/].

## Historical Context of Tariff Rates

Current tariff levels represent a dramatic departure from recent historical norms. Current tariff policy with Section 122 tariffs in effect represents an 11.3 percentage point increase in the U.S. average effective tariff rate on a pre-substitution basis [source: https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-february-21-2026]. Even if Section 122 tariffs expire as scheduled in 150 days, the effective tariff rate will be 9.1%, the highest since 1947 [source: https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-february-21-2026].

## Economic Impact on Businesses and Consumers

The tariff policies have imposed significant costs on American businesses and households. American households are paying an estimated $1,500 more per year due to tariffs [source: https://elevatewealth.ae/blog/effects-trump-tariffs-us-economy/]. Research from the New York Federal Reserve found that 86-94% of tariff costs fall on American firms and consumers rather than foreign exporters [source: https://elevatewealth.ae/blog/effects-trump-tariffs-us-economy/].

For manufacturers specifically, the financial impact has been substantial. Manufacturers importing $10 million in components annually face approximately $1 million in additional tariff costs without mitigation strategies [source: https://wiss.com/trump-tariffs-2026-financial-impact-on-us-manufacturers/]. Some manufacturers absorbed tariff costs rather than passing them to customers, directly reducing their profit margins [source: https://wiss.com/trump-tariffs-2026-financial-impact-on-us-manufacturers/]. Tariff management in 2026 affects cost structure, working capital, and competitive positioning beyond simple tax compliance [source: https://wiss.com/trump-tariffs-2026-financial-impact-on-us-manufacturers/].

The broader economic effects have also been negative according to some analyses. Trump tariffs reduced GDP growth by half a percentage point and resulted in half a million fewer jobs being created [source: https://elevatewealth.ae/blog/effects-trump-tariffs-us-economy/]. Paradoxically, the manufacturing sector lost over 100,000 jobs despite tariffs being designed to protect it [source: https://elevatewealth.ae/blog/effects-trump-tariffs-us-economy/].

## Corporate Adaptation and Strategic Shifts

One year after Trump's tariff push, companies continue to face the effects of changing policy [source: https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/03/trump-tariffs-trade-war-impact.html]. The past year of tariffs has culturally shifted the way U.S. companies operate [source: https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/03/trump-tariffs-trade-war-impact.html]. Companies have been forced to be more flexible and diversify their supply chains over the past year due to ever-changing trade and tariff policies [source: https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/03/trump-tariffs-trade-war-impact.html].

In some cases, the administration has offered targeted relief in exchange for specific commitments. Trump awarded pharmaceutical companies a three-year exemption from tariffs in exchange for price cuts and commitments to invest further in U.S. manufacturing [source: https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/03/trump-tariffs-trade-war-impact.html].

## Legal Implications and Refund Eligibility

The Supreme Court's invalidation of IEEPA tariffs has created potential financial remedies for affected businesses. If the Supreme Court invalidates IEEPA tariffs, manufacturers who paid these duties will be eligible for refunds [source: https://wiss.com/trump-tariffs-2026-financial-impact-on-us-manufacturers/]. U.S. business groups are calling for refunds following the Supreme Court's tariff ruling [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling].

## International Reactions and Trade Relations

Global responses to the tariff policy changes have varied significantly by country and region. Following the Supreme Court ruling and subsequent policy adjustments, some countries have seen their tariff burdens reduced. South Korean companies in chemicals, pharmaceuticals, and semiconductors would pay lower tariff rates under the new measures compared to previous policies [source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/21/trump-tariff-reactions]. India's tariffs were reduced to 18 percent and penal duties on Russian imports were removed following the Supreme Court ruling [source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/21/trump-tariff-reactions].

China, a primary target of Trump's tariff policies, responded cautiously. China's embassy in Washington issued a statement noting that trade wars benefit nobody, and the decision is likely to be broadly welcomed in China [source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/21/trump-tariff-reactions].

The legal uncertainty has complicated trade negotiations. Some foreign officials have stated their legislatures have not yet ratified recent trade deals with the U.S. that were designed with the now-voided tariffs in mind [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling]. Business chiefs and government leaders are analyzing the fallout from the Supreme Court decision, with international stakeholders reassessing their positions in response to the tariff policy changes [source: https://www.latimes.com/world-nation/story/2026-02-21/global-leaders-businesses-react-to-more-u-s-tariff-swings].

## EU-US Trade Dynamics

The relationship between the European Union and United States illustrates the evolving nature of tariff impacts. US tariffs increased sharply in 2025 but were less severe than initially feared [source: https://think.ing.com/articles/the-us-tariff-shock-same-negative-impact-different-drivers-in-2025-and-2026/]. The combined impact of direct tariffs, shifts in relative competitiveness, and uncertainty-related effects resulted in a net reduction of around 4.2% in EU-US exports in 2025 [source: https://think.ing.com/articles/the-us-tariff-shock-same-negative-impact-different-drivers-in-2025-and-2026/].

Looking to 2026, the projection assumes the conclusion of a trade agreement between the EU and the US [source: https://think.ing.com/articles/the-us-tariff-shock-same-negative-impact-different-drivers-in-2025-and-2026/]. However, despite a trade agreement, effectively paid tariffs are expected to increase slightly in 2026, with economic impacts strengthening gradually over time [source: https://think.ing.com/articles/the-us-tariff-shock-same-negative-impact-different-drivers-in-2025-and-2026/].

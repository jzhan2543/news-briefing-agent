Honest log of failure modes observed during development of the news briefing agent. Each entry: what happened, root cause (if known), mitigation status, and interview relevance.

Captured as observed, not reconstructed from memory. Not every entry is a bug — some are "plausible-looking but unverified" cases that motivate design choices downstream.

## Researcher: JSON parse failure on malformed LLM output

**Trace:** `f04ed178-0c42-4833-8dd1-d5313a3c50a4`
**Trace URL:** https://smith.langchain.com/o/18018a9f-c029-4d1d-b822-750c9d951724/projects/p/5bab7076-8548-48b6-94e3-2d0a0d161a5e?peek=20260420T004438Z019da858-b4c8-77a0-95ba-ea3379ecf837&peeked_trace=20260420T004438984092Z019da858-b4c8-77a0-95ba-ea3379ecf837
**When:** Day 5 morning, during Summarizer integration.
**Symptom:** `json.JSONDecodeError: Expecting ',' delimiter: line 5 column 337 (char 563)` bubbling up from `researcher_node._extract_articles`. Classified as `reason="unknown"` by `run_briefing`'s last-resort catch.

**Root cause:** Hand-rolled JSON extraction from the research sub-agent's final message. The prompt told the model to emit a raw JSON array; the model complied on most runs but occasionally produced technically-invalid JSON — almost certainly an unescaped double-quote inside an article `title` or `snippet`. `raw.find("[")` to `raw.rfind("]")` handles prose-wrapping but nothing enforces string-escape validity.

**Fix:** Replaced text parsing with `response_format=_ResearcherOutput` on `create_agent`. The sub-agent's final step is now a schema-coerced tool call, not free-form text, so the API enforces validity and `agent.invoke(...)` returns a typed Pydantic instance via `result["structured_response"]`. Same fix later applied proactively to Summarizer (`with_structured_output`) and Formatter.

**Interview relevance:** Concrete example of "moved validation to the API boundary where it's enforced, rather than after the fact where it's brittle." Also a concrete case where a recurring failure classified as `reason="unknown"` was promoted — exactly the evolution path flagged in the runner's docstring.

## Formatter: Plausible quote attribution that may not be faithful to source

**Trace:** `fd3f22a8-1e3a-4299-ab0f-43ddd79625b4`
**Topic:** "Fed interest rates decisions 2026"
**When:** Day 5 morning, second end-to-end validation run.
**Symptom:** Briefing contains this sentence under "Geopolitical Factors":

> The Fed's post-meeting statement noted 'uncertain' impacts from a war with Iran [source: https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/18/fed-interest-rate-decision-march-2026.html].

The attribution frames 'uncertain' as a direct quote from the Fed's post-meeting statement, but the cited source is the CNBC article, not the Fed statement itself. Two possibilities that the briefing alone cannot distinguish:
1. CNBC directly quoted the Fed, the Summarizer faithfully relayed the quote with its attribution, and the Formatter preserved it correctly. This is fine.
2. CNBC paraphrased the Fed, the Summarizer or Formatter introduced quote marks that weren't in the source snippet, and the result is a fabricated attribution. This is a faithfulness failure.

**Root cause:** Unknown without opening the trace and comparing the CNBC article's actual text against the Summarizer's per-article output. The pipeline has no current mechanism to verify that specific claims — especially quoted claims — trace back to the cited source.

**Mitigation status:** Not fixed. Flagged as a Day 6 eval target. The LLM-as-judge faithfulness check is the intended mechanism: "for each bracketed citation, does the preceding claim appear in the summary of the cited article?" Quoted claims warrant a stricter variant: "does this exact string appear in the snippet?"

**Interview relevance:** Real example of why post-hoc faithfulness evaluation matters. The briefing reads well, cites sources, follows the format — and still may contain a subtle fabrication. This is the gap between "looks correct" and "is correct" that motivates the Day 6 eval work. Also a concrete answer to "how would you evaluate an agent whose output is prose?" — you need a faithfulness judge, not just a schema check.

## Filter: Silent partial-retrieval degradation under rate-limit pressure

**Trace:** Not captured at the time — the degraded run's thread_id lived only in `metadata.json`, which was overwritten by the clean re-run before the trace ID was copied out. LangSmith's 14-day retention means the trace is recoverable by timestamp + topic if needed.
**Topic:** "Central bank interest rate decisions this quarter"
**When:** Day 6 morning, during the first single-agent pass of the eval harness (5 cells run sequentially in one invocation).
**Symptom:** Cell completed with `status=success`, `source_urls` length 6, `briefing_chars` 5,851. The other four single-agent cells in the same batch had 11-15 sources and 8-13k chars each. Terminal output showed six `[filter] scoring failed for <url>: Error code: 429` lines with the organization-level "30,000 input tokens per minute" message. All six scoring calls were for legitimate articles Tavily had returned; the articles were not dropped for irrelevance, they were dropped because their Filter calls were refused by the API.

**Root cause:** Filter node's `asyncio.gather` fan-out with `return_exceptions=True` (filter.py:135) treats rate-limit errors as per-article scoring failures rather than escalating them to the pipeline level. Correct posture for transient failures — the pipeline absorbs partial losses and moves on — but there's no mechanism to distinguish "this article was low-quality and got a low score" from "this article never got scored at all." Both end the same way: article not in `scored_articles`. The resulting briefing is shorter than it should be but passes every existing validation. Upstream root cause: the single-agent batch ran 4 cells back-to-back, each cell's Researcher + Filter fan-out pushed input token usage toward the 30k TPM sliding window, and the third cell's Filter was the one that crossed it.

**Mitigation status:** Not fixed in code. Worked around by splitting single-agent and multi-agent runs into separate invocations with pacing (`sleep 60` between multi-agent cells). Two orthogonal mitigations worth considering later: (a) promote `RateLimitError` out of the `return_exceptions=True` catch, classify it as a pipeline-level failure so cells with rate-limit victims are flagged rather than returned as degraded successes, (b) add a derived metric in the Day 6 afternoon work — "source count vs. expected range" — that surfaces asymmetric cells automatically without requiring per-error classification.

**Interview relevance:** Concrete Q9 (what to watch in traces) and Q13 (online eval without ground truth) example. Terminal status said success; `source_urls.length` said otherwise. The lesson is that derived signals — counts, distributions, trajectory shape — catch degradations that binary status flags miss. Also a concrete case where error-handling philosophy matters: `return_exceptions=True` is right for transient per-item failures and wrong when the error represents a systemic constraint that should be surfaced. Honest answer when asked "how would you fix this": I'd split the exception class — retry transient, escalate rate-limit. The project has this as a known-deferred item.

## Critic(articles): Silent article drop — agent returned N-1 scores for N inputs

**Trace:** Not captured at the time; thread_id is in `evals/results/current/uk_budget_2026/multi_agent/metadata.json` for the curious. LangSmith trace recoverable by topic + timestamp.
**Topic:** "UK Autumn Budget 2026 tax and spending changes"
**When:** Day 6 morning, during the multi-agent cell run (topic was the last in the paced multi-agent loop).
**Symptom:** Terminal output showed `[critic_articles] agent returned no score for article 13 (https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/ce8wew99ngpo); skipping`. The Critic(articles) agent was passed a raw article list (13 items); its `response_format` output contained scores for 12 of them and simply omitted the 13th. The node wrapper's defensive logging surfaces the discrepancy but doesn't raise — the pipeline continued with 12 scored articles, produced a 9-source briefing, returned `status=success`.

**Root cause:** `response_format` on `create_agent` enforces that the output *matches a schema* but doesn't enforce that the output *covers every input*. The Critic agent's prompt asks it to score "each article" in the input list, but the model has latitude to miss one — and because the wire format is a list of scored-article objects (not a dict keyed on input URL), there's no structural signal that an input was skipped. The node wrapper has to detect the mismatch by comparing input URLs to output URLs after the fact (the log line above is that comparison firing). Contrast with the Filter node, which dispatches one call per article and notices missing output per-article at the gather boundary; Critic(articles) is a single batched call and the loss surface is different.

**Mitigation status:** Not fixed. Two possible directions: (a) reshape the Critic's wire format from a list to a dict keyed on input URL, which makes missing inputs structurally detectable (and enforceable via schema validation); (b) add a post-condition check in the node wrapper — "if fewer scored articles came back than went in, retry once with tighter prompting or raise `schema_violation`." Option (a) is more invasive but closer to the right design. DESIGN.md §4 decision 3 ("wire format vs. state artifact separation") is the precedent — the wire format is already separate from the rich state artifact type, so reshaping the wire format is cheaper than it would be without that separation.

**Interview relevance:** This is the more *architectural* of the two Day 6 failure-mode discoveries. The rate-limit case is environmental — it wouldn't happen with a higher API tier. This case is about `response_format` being a necessary but insufficient guarantee. Schema validation is often oversold in agent pipelines as "this makes the output safe"; it makes the output's *shape* safe, not its *completeness* with respect to the input. Concrete Q14 (guardrails) talking point: schema guardrails are output-side only; pipeline completeness requires a separate post-condition check. Concrete Q5/Q8 (testing + golden dataset contents) talking point: for any agent that consumes a collection, I'd include an input-vs-output-cardinality check in the trajectory metric alongside more obvious things like tool-call counts.

## Tavily cache: Retrieval not actually pinned across pipelines despite cache hits

**Trace:** Not captured at the time; `thread_id` for both cells is in `evals/results/current/iphone_launch_rumors/{single_agent,multi_agent}/metadata.json`. Cell pair was the basis for the Day 6 afternoon qualitative read.
**Topic:** "iPhone 17 launch rumors and supply-chain reports"
**When:** Day 6 afternoon, during the side-by-side qualitative read of multi-agent vs. single-agent briefings in preparation for source-quality metric design.
**Symptom:** Single-agent `source_urls` contained `https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SNRzCTl3siU`; multi-agent `source_urls` did not contain it (and did not contain any URL from `youtube.com`). Same topic, same `run_id=current`, supposedly-pinned retrieval via the cache layer in `src/tools/tavily_cache.py`. Nothing in the terminal output flagged this — both cells completed with `status=success` and the harness doesn't compare retrieval sets across pipelines. The divergence was only caught because the afternoon plan involved reading both briefings side-by-side for the source-quality metric work; under normal eval operation it would have been invisible. The eu_ai_act_enforcement pair shows the same pattern: single-agent retrieved `sloanreview.mit.edu` and `kpmg.com`; multi-agent did not retrieve either.

**Root cause:** Hypothesized, not confirmed — the cache is keyed on the *query string passed to `web_search`*, not on the topic. That keying assumes planner queries are deterministic (they are: temperature 0.0 on `planner_node`) and that the researcher sub-agent's tool-call arguments match the planner queries verbatim (this is the suspect assumption). The researcher is a `create_agent` ReAct loop (researcher.py:59) that receives a planner query in its user message and decides what to pass to `web_search` on its own — it can rephrase, split, or narrow, and its tool-call arguments are not constrained to equal the planner query. Even at temperature 0.0, the sub-agent's message history diverges between the single-agent and multi-agent graphs because the upstream state differs (different nodes ran before, different state shape), so the sub-agent's first tool-call wording can differ → different cache key → different Tavily call → different article set → the rest of the pipeline diverges from there. Alternative plausible causes not ruled out: (a) case/punctuation differences the normalizer doesn't catch (the normalizer only lowercases and collapses whitespace; trailing `?`, Unicode variants, re-ordered clauses all bypass it), (b) the researcher issues follow-up `web_search` calls within one ReAct turn whose arguments depend on the prior call's results, so even identical first-calls produce divergent second-calls once the two pipelines' prior state differs, (c) non-determinism at the ChatAnthropic layer even with temperature 0.0, which has been observed in tied-logit cases.

**Mitigation status:** Partially mitigated — the symptom is now *measured*; the cause is not fixed. Day 7 AM shipped `evals/retrieval_overlap.py`, the "cheapest" mitigation from the three tiers below: per-topic Jaccard similarity on normalized `source_urls` sets, threshold 0.7, pass/fail per topic, details on below-threshold cells, output to `results/<run_id>/retrieval_overlap.json` for consumption by the aggregation pass. First run against `results/current/`: 3 of 5 topics pass (eu_ai_act 0.85, iphone 0.92, tariffs 0.93), 2 fail (central_bank 0.47, uk_budget 0.54). The failing cells revealed a pattern the qualitative read hadn't fully captured: on central_bank, single-agent retrieved UGC (LinkedIn) and thin-source content (`maseconomics.com`, `usbank.com`) while multi-agent retrieved institutional primary sources (JPMorgan, KPMG, StLouisFed, CNBC). That's not "Critic dropped bad sources" — it's "the researcher sub-agent issued different queries and hit different tiers of retrieval." The Critic-drop narrative only holds on cells where retrieval is actually pinned, i.e., the three passing topics. Known limitation of the invariant itself: URL normalization strips `www.` and trailing slash but not tracking query parameters, so same-article URLs differing only in `?srsltid=...` or similar would register as distinct; not observed on the current eval set but would bias Jaccard downward if encountered. Concrete mitigations still open by effort level: (a) — done this morning; (b) medium — log every `web_search` tool-call argument to disk during eval runs and diff them across pipelines, confirms whether the researcher is rephrasing or whether non-determinism at the model layer is the cause; (c) expensive and correct — move from query-keyed cache to topic-keyed fixed corpus (Option B from DESIGN.md §6 item 1). Recommended next: (b), ~30 min, would distinguish the researcher-rephrase hypothesis from the alternatives listed in root cause. Cross-pipeline metric claims in the Day 7 aggregation table: legitimate on the three passing topics, carry a retrieval-divergence caveat on central_bank and uk_budget. The cache is doing what it claims; the pinning claim has been narrowed from "across pipelines" to "per query within a pipeline."

**Interview relevance:** The "cache-works-but-pinning-doesn't" split is a cleaner version of the Critic(articles) `response_format` finding one entry up: in both cases, a necessary-but-insufficient guarantee was being treated as sufficient. Schema validation guarantees output *shape* not output *completeness*; query-keyed caching guarantees *replay determinism* not *cross-pipeline equivalence*. Concrete Q13 (evaluating agents without ground truth) talking point: the harness invariants you assert have to match the claims you make about the output. "I pinned retrieval" was a cross-pipeline equivalence claim; query-keyed caching only supports per-query replay determinism. The Jaccard module is the concrete "built a harness-level invariant after discovering the cache didn't guarantee what I was claiming" artifact — and the fact that running it revealed 2 of 5 topics below threshold, rather than decorating a clean all-green story, is the Q13-shaped point: invariants are valuable because they reveal gaps. Parallels the Critic(articles) `response_format` finding one entry up — both cases where a necessary-but-insufficient guarantee (shape vs. completeness; replay determinism vs. cross-pipeline equivalence) was treated as sufficient.

## Multi-agent flag amplification: low_credibility flag propagates into systematic viewpoint suppression

**Trace:** `e94738d9-72d1-4116-9766-69313ca33b0a`. Cell: `evals/results/current/eu_ai_act_enforcement/multi_agent/`.
**Topic:** "EU AI Act enforcement across member states"
**When:** Day 7, discovered during interview rehearsal for the EU AI Act cell — opened the trace to verify a claim I'd made about where the Critic dropped civil-society content, and found the actual mechanism was materially different.
**Symptom:** Multi-agent's final briefing contains zero mentions of EDRi (European Digital Rights), civil society, implementation delays, or the digital omnibus controversy, despite EDRi being one of the 15 articles retrieved by the Researcher and surviving the Critic(articles) relevance cut. The briefing's headline, "EU AI Act Enforcement Framework Takes Shape as Member States Designate National Authorities," is the opposite framing of single-agent's headline on the same topic: "Faces Implementation Delays as Member States Struggle to Designate Authorities." Part 2 of the LLM judge flagged 2 high-importance retrieval-utilization omissions on this cell; Part 1 scored the briefing at 100% claim support — the highest in the entire 10-cell eval.

**Root cause:** A multi-agent cascade, not a single-agent failure. Trace inspection of each span in order: (1) Critic(articles) evaluated EDRi and attached a `low_credibility(low)` flag — reasonable given EDRi is an advocacy NGO rather than a .gov or major legal publisher. EDRi was NOT filtered; it was passed through with a flag. (2) Writer's first draft cited EDRi twice with explicit hedge strings ("*though this assessment comes from civil society advocacy and has not been independently verified by regulatory bodies*") — this is defensible behavior, the Writer respected the flag by hedging rather than dropping. (3) Critic(draft) rejected the first draft with feedback that cited the `low_credibility(low)` flag verbatim and offered three remediation options: "omit, significantly caveat as coming from advocacy groups with uncertain verification, or balance with other sources if available." The Critic(draft) also flagged the Writer's own hedge strings as "Writer inventions" — ironic given those hedges were the Writer respecting the upstream flag. (4) Writer's second draft picked "omit." Every EDRi citation gone, every implementation-delays mention gone, headline re-framed to "Framework Takes Shape." (5) Critic(draft) accepted the second draft. Each agent behaved locally reasonably given its inputs; the composition produced systematic viewpoint suppression. The key mechanism: `low_credibility(low)` as a flag *name* reads like a verdict on the claim ("this claim is not credible"), not a description of the source ("this source is an advocacy NGO"). The Critic(draft) correctly read it as a reason to act; the three options it offered made omission the path of least resistance.

**Mitigation status:** Not fixed. Candidate fixes by scope: (a) cheapest — rename `low_credibility` to something descriptive rather than evaluative, e.g. `perspective(advocacy)` or `institutional_source(no)`. A label that describes the source without implying the claim is wrong changes how downstream agents treat it. (b) medium — tighten the Critic(draft) prompt: "if a flagged source is the only source for a perspective, do not offer omission as an option; offer attribution-with-hedge." (c) medium — tighten the Writer's revision prompt: "hedged citations are acceptable output; do not remove hedged content to avoid Critic(draft) rejection." (d) architectural — separate *source* flags from *claim* verdicts in the wire format. Flags describe the source (advocacy, press release, paywall); verdicts apply to specific claims (unsupported, contradicts-other-source). Collapsing the two is what allowed flag-as-signal to become flag-as-mandate. Any of these would break the cascade; (a) is the minimum-touch change and the one I'd try first. Cost budget for verifying a fix: re-run the multi-agent EU AI Act cell under the changed config and re-read the briefing; the Jaccard cache means retrieval stays pinned, so the change is isolated to the Critic→Writer→Critic path.

**Interview relevance:** This is the best multi-agent failure-mode finding the project has. The shape is "flag amplification across agents" — one agent's cautionary signal becomes the next agent's mandate. Each agent is locally right, and the trajectory is systematically biased. That's exactly the class of failure mode that multi-agent systems are supposed to introduce and that single-agent systems structurally can't, and it's something I have trace-level evidence for rather than theory for. Concrete Q2 (multi-agent systems) talking point: the value-vs-cost story isn't "multi-agent adds tokens" — it's "multi-agent adds inter-agent-composition failure modes that neither agent would produce in isolation." Concrete Q3 (sequential execution) talking point: the revision loop's force-accept-at-cap behavior doesn't help here because the cascade converges at the second draft; the Critic(draft) accepts because its own instructions are being followed. Concrete Q12 (LLM-as-judge) talking point: the Critic(draft) as automated reviewer has the exact same self-preference-at-composition risk as an eval-time judge, because Claude reading Claude's flag and instructing Claude to remove content is a consensus mechanism, not an adversarial check. Concrete Q11 (HITL) talking point: swapping Critic(draft) for a human would have caught this — a human reviewer would either have kept the hedge or overridden the flag, because humans reading "low_credibility(low)" against a 31-organization civil-society open letter would notice the mismatch. The automated reviewer took the flag at face value. This is one of the cleanest concrete cases in my project for "when multi-agent adds risk" rather than "when multi-agent adds cost."

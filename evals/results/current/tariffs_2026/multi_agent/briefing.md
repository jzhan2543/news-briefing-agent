# US Tariff Policy in 2026: Supreme Court Ruling, Policy Adjustments, and Economic Impacts

## Executive Summary

US tariff policy in 2026 has been shaped by a landmark Supreme Court ruling that invalidated tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), the Trump administration's subsequent pivot to alternative tariff authorities, and ongoing negotiations under the Agreement on Reciprocal Trade (ART) program [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling]. The average effective US tariff rate stands at 15.8% as of recent estimates, down from a peak of 22% on "Liberation Day" but significantly higher than the 2.3% rate at the end of 2024 [source: https://www.jpmorgan.com/insights/global-research/current-events/us-tariffs]. These policy shifts have created substantial economic impacts including price increases for consumer goods, supply chain disruptions, and potential refunds for businesses that paid now-invalidated tariffs [source: https://www.cnn.com/interactive/2026/business/tracking-trump-tariff-impact-us/].

## Supreme Court Ruling and Administrative Response

The Supreme Court issued a 6-3 ruling striking down many of Trump's tariffs that had been imposed under IEEPA, deeming them illegal [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling]. Following this decision, the White House moved to preserve tariff levies under other legal authorities [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling]. The ruling has created eligibility for refunds: manufacturers who paid IEEPA-based duties can now seek reimbursement, though the outlook for actually receiving refunds is negative according to some analysts [source: https://wiss.com/trump-tariffs-2026-financial-impact-on-us-manufacturers/] [source: https://capstonedc.com/insights/trade-2026-preview/]. Multiple US business groups have called for refunds to begin [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling]. The administration has deployed a backup plan to impose tariffs through alternative authorities following the Court's decision [source: https://capstonedc.com/insights/trade-2026-preview/].

## Current Tariff Framework and Trade Policy Priorities

The 2026 Trade Policy Agenda outlines six core areas for the America First Trade Policy: continuing the Agreement on Reciprocal Trade (ART) Program, pursuing robust enforcement of trade agreements and US trade laws, securing supply chains for critical minerals and sectors, conducting the USMCA review, managing trade with China for reciprocity and balance, and promoting American interests in international fora [source: https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20Trade%20Policy%20Agenda.pdf]. The administration has taken a tailored approach to Section 232 tariffs, imposing them on a smaller portion of goods than those covered by the scope of investigations [source: https://capstonedc.com/insights/trade-2026-preview/]. Tariff policy includes comprehensive measures covering steel, aluminum, copper, automobiles, pharmaceuticals, semiconductors, lumber, and furniture, along with reciprocal tariffs and closure of the de minimis exemption [source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariffs_in_the_second_Trump_administration].

The US Trade Representative announced new Section 301 investigations into 16 countries and regions including China, the European Union, Singapore, Switzerland, Norway, Indonesia, Malaysia, Cambodia, Thailand, Korea, Vietnam, Taiwan, Bangladesh, Mexico, Japan, and India [source: https://spectrumlocalnews.com/us/snplus/business/2026/03/12/president-trump-tariffs-section-301-investigations-16-countries-european-union]. A docket to collect comments on these investigations opened in March, with hearings scheduled for May [source: https://spectrumlocalnews.com/us/snplus/business/2026/03/12/president-trump-tariffs-section-301-investigations-16-countries-european-union].

## Trade Agreement Negotiations

The US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) is expected to be renewed by the end of Q2 2026, with bilateral deals addressing some Section 232 tariffs [source: https://capstonedc.com/insights/trade-2026-preview/]. The administration may reduce or eliminate reciprocal tariffs and certain Section 232 tariffs for trading partners that conclude final trade and security agreements with the US [source: https://dimerco.com/us-tariff-update-2026/] — as reported by Dimerco, though this is a commercial logistics source rather than an official government announcement. The Supreme Court ruling has emboldened US trade partners, with some foreign officials publicly stating their legislatures have not yet ratified recent trade deals with Washington that were crafted with the now-voided tariffs in mind [source: https://www.cfr.org/articles/countries-react-to-scotus-tariff-ruling].

## Economic and Business Impacts

Businesses have reported that tariff policies will likely lead to price increases and labor market impacts in 2026 [source: https://equitablegrowth.org/u-s-businesses-report-that-tariff-policies-will-likely-lead-to-price-increases-and-labor-market-impacts-in-2026/]. Firms expressed pessimism in the late April Federal Reserve Beige Book, with some producers planning to pass through tariff costs to consumers and shortening pricing windows for business clients in response to existing and anticipated tariffs [source: https://equitablegrowth.org/u-s-businesses-report-that-tariff-policies-will-likely-lead-to-price-increases-and-labor-market-impacts-in-2026/]. For manufacturers importing $10 million in components annually, additional tariff costs now approximate $1 million, assuming no mitigation strategies [source: https://wiss.com/trump-tariffs-2026-financial-impact-on-us-manufacturers/]. Some manufacturers absorbed tariff costs rather than passing them to customers, directly reducing profit margins [source: https://wiss.com/trump-tariffs-2026-financial-impact-on-us-manufacturers/].

Heavily imported consumer goods have seen some of the largest price increases due to higher tariffs, particularly those with lower profit margins that make absorbing tariffs difficult [source: https://www.cnn.com/interactive/2026/business/tracking-trump-tariff-impact-us/]. Trump's sweeping tariffs sent shockwaves across the global economy, fracturing longstanding alliances and forcing businesses to rewrite their operational strategies [source: https://www.cnn.com/interactive/2026/business/tracking-trump-tariff-impact-us/]. Companies have been forced to be more flexible and diversify their supply chains in response to ever-changing trade and tariff policies [source: https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/03/trump-tariffs-trade-war-impact.html] — though this CNBC article is flagged as moderately outdated and may reflect earlier 2026 conditions.

## International Trade Effects

US tariffs are projected to reduce euro area GDP by 0.3% in 2026 alone [source: https://www.reuters.com/world/china/eu-trade-surplus-shrinks-60-us-exports-fall-due-tariffs-2026-04-17/]. With Chinese tariffs reaching punitive rates, even conservative estimates suggest China's share of US imports should shrink dramatically, which would reduce the increase in total average effective tariffs [source: https://www.jpmorgan.com/insights/global-research/current-events/us-tariffs]. Analysts project that greater policy certainty in 2026 compared to 2025, combined with global statutory tariff levels converging toward 15%, will shape trade flows differently than the initial tariff shock period [source: https://think.ing.com/articles/the-us-tariff-shock-same-negative-impact-different-drivers-in-2025-and-2026/].

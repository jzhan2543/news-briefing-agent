# EU AI Act Enforcement Faces Implementation Delays as Member States Struggle to Designate Authorities

## Executive Summary

The EU AI Act, adopted in 2024 to harmonize markets and promote trustworthy AI, faces significant implementation challenges as member states struggle to meet enforcement deadlines and designate required national authorities [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/]. Despite an August 2, 2025 deadline for establishing national competent authorities, almost no member state has fulfilled these obligations, prompting warnings from civil society organizations that the rights promised by the Act remain hollow without proper implementation [source: https://edri.org/our-work/open-letter-european-commission-member-states-keep-ai-act-national-implementation-on-track/] [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory]. Member states are adopting varied enforcement approaches, with some like France choosing decentralized models across multiple sector-specific authorities, while the European Commission pursues further centralization through a proposed digital omnibus [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/] [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/].

## Implementation Delays and Compliance Gaps

The enforcement of the EU AI Act has encountered serious obstacles as member states fail to meet critical implementation milestones. All EU member states were required to designate or establish their national competent authorities by August 2, 2025 [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory]. However, almost no member state has fulfilled their obligations to enact the AI Act and designate competent national authorities despite the deadline expiring [source: https://edri.org/our-work/open-letter-european-commission-member-states-keep-ai-act-national-implementation-on-track/].

This implementation gap has prompted significant concern from civil society. Thirty-one civil society organizations have called on the Commission and member states to act immediately to ensure timely national implementation, warning that without proper implementation, the rights enshrined in the AI Act remain hollow promises [source: https://edri.org/our-work/open-letter-european-commission-member-states-keep-ai-act-national-implementation-on-track/]. The limited progress is reflected in official tracking: only nine authorities appear in the Commission's consolidated list as National Competent Authorities under Articles 28 and 70, and just three authorities appear as authorities protecting fundamental rights under Article 77 [source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/national-implementation-plans/].

## Decentralized Enforcement Structure and National Authority Requirements

The EU AI Act employs a fundamentally decentralized enforcement model, empowering member states to establish their own rules on penalties and administrative fines [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. Under this framework, each member state must designate at least one national authority to oversee implementation and conduct market surveillance [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. More specifically, each member state must appoint at least one market surveillance authority and at least one notifying authority, though more than one of each can be designated [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory].

The terminology surrounding these authorities requires clarification. The term 'national competent authority' in the AI Act is an overarching collective term that encompasses both notifying authorities and market surveillance authorities, not a separate standalone authority [source: https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/who-governs-ai-eu-breakdown-authorities-eu-ai-act]. Each EU member state must establish or designate at least one notifying authority and at least one market surveillance authority as a single point of contact for AI Act purposes, with these authorities working in tandem to enforce the Act [source: https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/who-governs-ai-eu-breakdown-authorities-eu-ai-act]. National market surveillance authorities are among the most critical actors in the enforcement structure of the AI Act [source: https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/who-governs-ai-eu-breakdown-authorities-eu-ai-act].

Member states have flexibility in how they structure these authorities: they can establish new bodies or designate already existing national bodies as their national competent authorities for EU AI Act implementation and enforcement [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory]. The European Commission maintains a list of single points of contact for all Member States regarding AI Act enforcement [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/].

## Varied National Implementation Approaches

Member states are adopting diverse strategies for implementing the AI Act's enforcement requirements. France provides a notable example of a decentralized approach: the country is not planning to modify its national laws to adapt them to the EU AI Act but is instead drafting a national law to designate enforcement and oversight powers [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/] [source: https://www.technologyslegaledge.com/2025/11/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-implementation-in-key-member-states/]. France has adopted a decentralized enforcement model with multiple market surveillance authorities split by sector and type of AI system [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/] [source: https://www.technologyslegaledge.com/2025/11/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-implementation-in-key-member-states/].

Under France's sector-specific model, the French data protection authority (CNIL) will enforce prohibited AI systems related to emotion recognition in workplaces and educational institutions, while ANSM will oversee high-risk AI systems related to medical devices [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/] [source: https://www.technologyslegaledge.com/2025/11/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-implementation-in-key-member-states/]. Ireland has similarly designated 15 authorities for AI Act enforcement with a National AI Office to be established by August 2, 2026 [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/].

In Germany, the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action and the Ministry for Justice are jointly responsible for implementing the AI Act [source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/national-implementation-plans/]. At the EU level, the European Data Protection Supervisor is designated as the competent authority for AI systems used by EU institutions, agencies, offices and bodies [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory].

## Centralization Efforts and the Digital Omnibus Controversy

While member states pursue varied implementation strategies, the European Commission is simultaneously working to centralize certain enforcement functions. The digital omnibus on AI, proposed by the Commission in November 2025, is a set of amendments designed to further centralize enforcement of the AI Act [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/].

However, this centralization effort has generated controversy. Civil society organizations warn that the European Commission's planned digital omnibus threatens to deregulate and delay the implementation of the AI Act [source: https://edri.org/our-work/open-letter-european-commission-member-states-keep-ai-act-national-implementation-on-track/]. This tension between centralization and timely implementation represents a key challenge in the Act's enforcement trajectory.

For general-purpose AI models specifically, both national supervisory authorities and the new EU AI Office will have enforcement powers under the EU AI Act [source: https://cdp.cooley.com/the-eu-ai-act-key-milestones-compliance-challenges-and-the-road-ahead/]. GPAI model providers must maintain up-to-date documentation and share it with both the EU AI Office and downstream system providers [source: https://cdp.cooley.com/the-eu-ai-act-key-milestones-compliance-challenges-and-the-road-ahead/].

## Penalty Framework and Financial Consequences

The AI Act establishes a tiered fine structure based on the severity of violations [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. The penalty structure ranges from relatively minor infractions to the most serious violations. Providing incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information carries penalties up to €7,500,000 or 1% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act]. Non-compliance with prohibitions on AI systems carries the highest fine under the EU AI Act at up to €35,000,000 or 7% of annual worldwide turnover [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act].

These penalties represent a significant escalation in EU regulatory enforcement. Maximum fines under the EU AI Act exceed the GDPR's penalty cap of €20 million [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. The EU AI Act penalties exceed those under GDPR, making them among the heftiest penalties for non-compliance in the EU [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act].

## Compliance Support and Organizational Readiness

To facilitate compliance with the new regulatory framework, the European Commission has launched transitional support mechanisms. The Commission has launched the AI Pact, a voluntary initiative to support future implementation of the AI Act and encourage early compliance from AI providers and deployers in Europe and beyond [source: https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai]. The GPAI Code of Practice is a voluntary compliance tool created by independent experts that provides practical guidance for providers to meet AI Act obligations, specifically addressing compliance requirements related to transparency, copyright, and safety and security under the AI Act [source: https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai].

Transparency is a cornerstone of the EU AI Act [source: https://cdp.cooley.com/the-eu-ai-act-key-milestones-compliance-challenges-and-the-road-ahead/]. The EU AI Act features a phased implementation approach with a Code of Practice anticipated for general-purpose AI models [source: https://cdp.cooley.com/the-eu-ai-act-key-milestones-compliance-challenges-and-the-road-ahead/].

Organizational readiness for compliance varies significantly. An organization's readiness for EU AI Act compliance depends on how well it is already prepared in underlying compliance fields such as privacy and cybersecurity [source: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/organizations-face-challenges-in-timely-compliance-with-the-eu-ai-act/]. Leading organizations, particularly in highly regulated industries, already have the foundations in place to meet compliance requirements including governance, definitions, and tooling, and established players with compliance teams should be able to manage EU AI Act requirements effectively [source: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/organizations-face-challenges-in-timely-compliance-with-the-eu-ai-act/]. However, companies that are already behind in their AI journeys may find it daunting to navigate the new EU AI Act regulations [source: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/organizations-face-challenges-in-timely-compliance-with-the-eu-ai-act/].

## Extraterritorial Reach and Global Impact

The AI Act's enforcement extends beyond EU borders to affect international companies. The EU AI Act applies to any business that operates in the EU and offers AI products, services, or systems that can be used within the EU [source: https://kpmg.com/us/en/articles/2024/how-eu-ai-act-affects-us-based-companies.html]. The Act creates a framework for understanding the risk associated with AI and sets requirements for high-risk systems [source: https://kpmg.com/us/en/articles/2024/how-eu-ai-act-affects-us-based-companies.html].

For compliance, companies should review existing AI applications and categorize them to identify high-risk applications that require compliance with the EU AI Act [source: https://kpmg.com/us/en/articles/2024/how-eu-ai-act-affects-us-based-companies.html]. Automated detection solutions, intake questionnaires, and workflow platforms can accelerate the discovery, inventory, and classification activities needed for compliance mapping [source: https://kpmg.com/us/en/articles/2024/how-eu-ai-act-affects-us-based-companies.html].

## Foundational Objectives

The AI Act was adopted by the European Parliament and the Council in 2024 to improve the functioning of the internal market, promote human-centric and trustworthy AI, ensure protection against harmful AI systems, and support innovation [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/]. These objectives underpin the entire enforcement framework, though their realization depends on member states successfully implementing the required national structures and authorities.

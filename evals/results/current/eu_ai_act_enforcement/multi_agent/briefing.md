# EU AI Act Enforcement Framework Takes Shape as Member States Designate National Authorities

The EU AI Act, adopted in 2024, establishes a decentralized enforcement framework requiring each member state to designate national competent authorities by August 2, 2025 [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory]. The Act aims to improve the functioning of the internal market, promote human-centric and trustworthy AI, ensure protection against harmful effects of AI systems, and support innovation [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/]. The European Commission maintains a list of single points of contact for all member states, with the digital omnibus proposal put forward in November 2025 seeking to further centralize enforcement [source: https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/18/enforcement-of-the-ai-act/].

## Enforcement Structure and Authority Designation

Each member state must establish or designate at least one market surveillance authority and at least one notifying authority, though countries may appoint multiple authorities to match their organizational needs [source: https://iapp.org/resources/article/eu-ai-act-regulatory-directory]. The term 'national competent authority' serves as an overarching collective term encompassing both notifying authorities and market surveillance authorities [source: https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/who-governs-ai-eu-breakdown-authorities-eu-ai-act]. Market surveillance authorities represent one of the most critical actors in the enforcement structure [source: https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/who-governs-ai-eu-breakdown-authorities-eu-ai-act].

The Commission's consolidated list shows varying levels of authority designation across member states: nine authorities appear for one member state, three for another, and twenty for a third [source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/national-implementation-plans/]. Both national supervisory authorities and the new EU AI Office will have enforcement powers, particularly for general-purpose AI models [source: https://cdp.cooley.com/the-eu-ai-act-key-milestones-compliance-challenges-and-the-road-ahead/].

## Member State Implementation Approaches

France has adopted a decentralized model with multiple market surveillance authorities divided by sector and AI system type [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/]. The French data protection supervisory authority (CNIL) will enforce prohibitions on emotion recognition AI systems in workplaces and educational institutions, while the National Agency for the Safety of Medicines and Health Products (ANSM) will oversee high-risk AI systems related to medical devices [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/]. France is not planning to modify its national laws to adapt them to the EU AI Act but has moved to draft national legislation designating enforcement and oversight powers [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/].

Ireland has designated 15 authorities, with a National AI Office to be established by August 2, 2026 [source: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/state-of-the-act-eu-ai-act-3732095/]. In Germany, the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action and the Ministry for Justice are jointly responsible for implementation, with sources suggesting the Federal Network Agency will be designated as market surveillance authority and the Federal Accreditation body as notifying authority [source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/national-implementation-plans/].

## Penalty Framework

The AI Act empowers member states to establish their own rules on penalties, including administrative fines for infringements [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. The penalty structure is divided into levels based on breach severity, with fines exceeding those provided under the General Data Protection Regulation [source: https://www.euaiact.com/key-issue/1]. The heftiest fines apply to using or making available prohibited AI systems, with penalties reaching up to €35,000,000 or 7% of annual worldwide turnover, whichever is higher [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act]. Lower penalties of up to €7,500,000 or 1% of total worldwide annual turnover apply to providing incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information [source: https://www.holisticai.com/blog/penalties-of-the-eu-ai-act].

## Voluntary Compliance Initiatives

The Commission has launched the AI Pact, a voluntary initiative supporting future implementation and inviting AI providers and deployers from Europe and beyond to comply with key AI Act obligations ahead of mandatory deadlines [source: https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai]. The GPAI Code of Practice, submitted to the Commission by independent experts, offers practical guidance to help providers comply with obligations related to transparency, copyright, and safety and security [source: https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai]. General-purpose AI model providers must maintain up-to-date documentation and share it with both the EU AI Office and downstream system providers [source: https://cdp.cooley.com/the-eu-ai-act-key-milestones-compliance-challenges-and-the-road-ahead/].
